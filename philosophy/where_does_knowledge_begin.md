### Where does knowledge begin, and where does it lead?

> Have you ever pondered where knowledge actually comes from?

Mathematics or other scientific disciplines rely heavily on "a priori" knowledge. We often see mathematical definitions building upon previous ones. Consider the concept of a vector space. We define it as an Abelian group accompanied by scalar multiplication. This may prompt the question, "What's an Abelian group then?" It is essentially a group possessing a specific property. Diving even further, we come to define a group as a set enriched with certain structures.
But how do we define a set precisely? A straightforward answer is that we don't define a set; In order to build knowledge, we must start somewhere. Thus, there are always fundamental concepts that aren't defined in terms of anything else.
In math, these basic building blocks are often called foundations.

Yet the choice of these foundational axioms can be somewhat arbitrary and is often a subject of debate among mathematicians. Does this mean our knowledge is floating on a shaky ground?
And if these basic concepts aren't defined using other concepts, how can we truly understand their essence? How do we navigate the rigorous logical path that math demands? Relying solely on intuition isn't adequate. After all, your intuition may well differ from mine, leaving us without an objective method to resolve any disagreements.

This scenario throws light on intriguing questions about the origin and grounding of knowledge. And it echoes what's known as the Münchhausen trilemma, a thought experiment that illustrates the dilemma of justifying knowledge. Any attempt to prove any truth must be based either on circular reasoning, an infinite regress, or axiomatic assumptions - none of which provide a firm, unquestionable foundation.

One common approach to address disagreements when defining a mathematical structure is to list its properties, instead of attempting to pinpoint the exact nature of the structure (that's probably why you see an abundance of properties scattered throughout your college math notes). However, this approach is not without flaws; it's important to note that just because two structures adhere to the same set of properties, it doesn't necessarily mean they are the same (or in a looser mathematical sense, isomorphic).

That said, it's these unavoidable problems and unanswerable questions that drive humans to explore, learn, and create. So next time when you encounter a complex mathematical or scientific concept, consider the incredible journey of thought it took to get there. And who knows? You might just get inspired to add your own ideas to this vast edifice of human knowledge.
