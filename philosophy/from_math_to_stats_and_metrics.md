### A journey through mathematics, statistics, and metrics

Pure mathematics is a realm of ideals. It’s a realm that I can innately appreciate, but as a result, I also recognize its limitations. Take for example, the number 1 as a representation of an absolute, static unity. In the world of pure math, 1 is exactly one. But in reality, nothing remains perfectly unchanging, making the precise notatioin of “one” impossible. Herein lies the elegance of probability. It takes the fixed entity of one and transforms it into a variable, an entity that can be one but also may not be one. To appreciate this nuance, one must first delve into the foundations of mathematics to grasp the limitations of these ideal constructs.

In our complex universe, accurately determining the exact probability of any event is unattainable due to inherent randomness and an overwhelming number of uncontrollable variables. And therein lies the intrigue of statistics. In statistics, one must not only comprehend the underlying mathematics, but also be adept at navigating the complexities and uncertainties of reality that pure math alone is unable to resolve.

This, however, doesn't imply that we should study statistics in isolation. Solely relying on formulas without comprehending the underlying logic can be perilous. For example, in quantitative finance, one popular metric is Value at Risk (VaR), a percentile of a probability distribution of P&L. VaR has many shortcomings, e.g. it overlooks the tail behavior in a probability distribution, ironically, where the most critical risk information resides. These weaknesses significantly contributed to the 2008 financial crisis, leading to widespread skepticism about mathematical models at the time. Improved versions of VaR, like cVaR and ES, have since been developed, but many people persist in using VaR these days because it usually suffices – a poignant reflection of human nature.

This mirrors many machine learning metrics that are developed to assess a model's efficacy. These metrics, however, are not divine oracles; they are human constructs and can be potentially flawed. Sometimes, they might even be biased to favor certain models. The crafting of such metrics is indeed a complex task - it's no surprise that the development of a novel metric can be considered a significant academic achievement. In my experience, the nuanced problems we encounter are often beyond the reach of many existing metrics. This is where qualitative analysis and case-by-case interpretation come into play.

The takeaway is that we shouldn't blindly rely on numeric values produced by metrics alone. A comprehensive understanding is critical, including knowledge of what the metrics measure, their limitations, and an awareness of the specific context of our problems. It's not enough to simply create and apply models; the pursuit of research requires a more holistic understanding than what has been currently done.
