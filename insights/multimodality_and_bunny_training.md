### The use of Multimodality in Bunny Disciplining

As pet owners, we often resort to a straightforward "no" when trying to instill discipline in our pets when they are behaving inappropriately. I've employed this tactic many times when my bunny started chewing on furniture, only to find it largely ineffective. The problem lies in our pets' comprehension of our verbal reprimands.

Let's consider why: _do animals genuinely understand the word “no”?_ While I can't vouch for more cognitively advanced animals like dogs, when it comes to bunnies, the answer is an unequivocal no. It's not the literal interpretation of "no" that registers, but rather the tone we use.

Here's a glimpse into my usual interaction with my little bun: _“No, sweetie, you shouldn’t chew on this. It could be harmful to you”_, I would explain, accompanying my words with affectionate petting. When my repeated gentle admonitions fall on deaf ears (or rather, indifferent bunny ears), my frustration might get the better of me.
Rather, it's best to use a firm tone to convey a clear, serious message (this is something I am still practicing to perfect). For animals, the specific words we use often take a backseat to the auditory cues like pitch and tone that they pick up.

However, sometimes even the right tone isn't enough to discourage our friends from indulging in unwanted behavior. In these instances, I've found the use of a few concurrent methods to be helpful in reinforcing the “no” message. For bunnies, a common disciplinary technique is to press on their noses. This simple action asserts your dominance. This technique specifically leverages tactile sensation.
For the particular issue of chewing, I’ve also tried spraying bitter apple spray on objects that my bunny is attracted to, the unpleasant smell acts as a deterrent, stimulating the olfactory sensation. Then, paired with a firmly voiced “no”, the likelihood of conveying the message that she should refrain from chewing improves.

I hope this gives you an understanding of the “multimodal” approach to pet training that I've tried. They've worked a few times but not always. I'll probably update this if and when I gather enough statistical evidence to validate/invalidate its effectiveness.
